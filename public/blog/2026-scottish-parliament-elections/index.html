To be a bit serious for a moment, I actually got a bit invested in this whole shabang, which makes me a bit of a rare case in this election cycle. Turnout for 2026 was below average for Scottish Parliamentary Elections and certainly below the record turnout seen in the prior election in 2021. Pollsters and other people far more versed in this subject than I attribute this to apathetic and alienated voter sentiment.

The System

When voters show up to the polls in Scotland, they are given two ballots. The lilac ballot paper contains a list of all candidates standing for election in the given constituency. There are 73 of these classic, hyper-local officials elected to represent a specific location by classic first-past-the-post tallies. The peach ballot paper then contains a list of all political parties standing in the election. These ballots are used to elect seven members for each of eight regions. These members are allocated according to the d'Hondt Method, which divides the number of votes each party received on the peach ballot paper by one plus the number of constituency seats that party won in the region, then iteratively awarding seats according to who has the highest number of votes per currently allocated seats. Voters mark each ballot paper with a single "X" (I refuse to call it a cross as the Brits do) in the box next to the name of their preferred candidate/party.

This makes Scottish Parliament a flavor of Mixed-Member Proportional Representation, specifically an additional member flavor of MMP.

Why It Is Better Than Pure First-Past-the-Post

As an electoral system for a representative democracy, this is an improvement on first-past-the-post in terms of both representation and democracy. Starting with the democracy side, voter registration is more inclusive. Given the whole stink we as Americans raised about taxation without representation, it should be pretty embarassing that our former tyrants are beating us in ensuring anyone that pays taxes has a say in who decides what those taxes should be. The process for registering also seems a bit simpler; most voters will be able to register with the submission of a single, online form. Since I did not have an NHS number thing, I had to send in an alternate method of identification after the fact, but the whole process was much smoother than my experiences voting in the state of Texas.

There is some attempt to get rid of the spoiler effect and produce a parliamentary composition that (somewhat) reflects the views of the population that parliament is meant to represent. In comparison to first-past-the-post systems, the fact that there is literally any consideration at all to what the final composition of govenrment looks like in terms of representing how people voted is an improvement. At least in theory, this form of representative democracy at least pretends to be representative and democratic.

Why It Is Still Quite Bad

Unfortunately, this system still fails at adequately representing the will of the people as it were. Because there remains such a large emphasis on geography rather than people, you end up with a final composition in the national parliament that does not reflect the composition of the national voter base.

However, I would argue the biggest flaw of this system, at least the biggest flaw I observed in this cycle, is that it fails to eliminate the spoiler effect and the general need for voters to cast their votes strategically rather than ideologically. Because voters mark preference for exactly one candidate and one party, a vote for a more ideologically pure Ralph Nader still leaves the ideologically-preferable Al Gore short in Florida and leads to a George W. presidency, albeit this is manifest on the local level 73 times over. In Edinburgh Central terms, having the ideologically-similar (broad strokes, anyway) Angus Robertson and Lorna Jane Slater standing concurrently risked splitting the progressive, independence vote sufficiently to instead send Scottish Labour candidate James William Dalgleish to Holyrood, in spite of the fact that together former-MSP Robertson and MSP Slater carried over half of the votes cast in the constituency.

Parties are clearly aware of this as well. Anecdotally, the posters set up by parties at my local polling place, meant to win over whoever showed up on the fence and undecided about how to cast their vote, almost unanimously made the argument that a vote for our party is the only way to stop other party. Clearly this bore out across the nation, as observed by analysts on the returns broadcast who constantly noted the uninspiring platforms laid out across the board. I would argue it contributed to the apathetic voter sentiment; people are more motivated to vote for something they believe in than against something they do not.

The most frustrating thing about this system is that it was designed by unionist parties for partisan advantage. Ranked-choice voting was on the table, and it was rejected because Labour and the Tories were afraid they would lose on merit to the SNP
